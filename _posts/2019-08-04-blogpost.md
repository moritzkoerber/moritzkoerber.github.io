# Business Understanding

[Airbnb](airbnb.com) is a viable alternative to hotels, hostels, and suchlike for many travelers. However, since this industry is a saturated market, businesses must not fall behind in any aspect of their business, be it price or customer satisfaction. Although Airbnb does not directly offer accommodation to customers, the company can choose what hosts are featured on their website. Hosts that hold either many listings or list their estate frequently may be less personal and, thereby, possibly less welcoming in their communication. 

If this turns out to be true, we could get a feeling for the experience of our next trip with Airbnb prior to the trip and choose an accommodation that makes us happy. On the other hand, Airbnb could either feature these hosts less frequently or implement codes of conduct especially for these hosts to foster a customer friendly experience. 

In this blog post, I investigate this conjecture by means of Airbnb data from Boston and Seattle. To be more precise, I have a look at the data to examine

1. whether offering more accommodations is associated with a lower review score
2. whether offering accommodations for a longer time is associated with a lower review score
3. whether the prediction can be improved by including other features.

The analyzed dataset contains listings of homestays in Boston, MA and Seattle, WA on [airbnb.com](https://www.airbnb.com). More details and a download link of the data set can be found [here](https://www.kaggle.com/airbnb/boston) and [here](https://www.kaggle.com/airbnb/seattle/data). 

# Data Understanding

Airbnb provides a total review score that is a weighted score of aspects such as communication, location or value. They also collect data on the accommodation such as its type (apartment, house, etc.) and on the host such as the number of the host's listings and the availability of the listing in the past. Based on the research questions above, I will examine if the review score is influence by the number of the host's listings and the availability of the listing, and, if not, whether we can predict the review score at all.

# Recommendations based on the insights

<figure style="width: 35%;  margin: 1.5rem auto 2rem;">
  <img src="/resources/images/2019-08-04-blogpost/score.jpg"/>
</figure>

It seems that a host's behavior can influence the rating but not much. To come back to the research questions from the beginning: The fewer listings a host has and the fewer times he/she lists her/his property, the better the rating. This fits well with the fact that hosts with a higher response rate obtain better ratings. It seems that, besides other factors, hosts that offer quality instead of quantity provide a better experience. Hence, Airbnb may feature these hosts first in their search results. There is still room for improvement in the prediction. Airbnb is, therefore, also advised to include other features to examine what really drives the review scores.
